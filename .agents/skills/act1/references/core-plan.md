# Core Plan

Use this note for the big Act 1 question: what does the deck need to stop being a starter deck?

## What Act 1 Actually Tests

- bad opening hands
- weak damage density
- no answer to early multi-enemy fights
- over-greedy pathing before the deck has earned it
- boss preparation that starts too late

## Draft Priorities

In most runs, Act 1 rewards this order:

1. reliable frontload
2. vulnerable or damage conversion
3. draw or energy smoothing
4. AoE or board-control help
5. compactness through removes or transforms

## Practical Rule

- A card is good in Act 1 if it improves the next few floors, not if it points at a future deck that does not exist yet.
- Respect skip when the offered cards are just more medium turns.
- Starter health is permission to invest in deck quality, not permission to leak HP carelessly in hallways.
- Act 1 should not only add cards; it should identify what the deck will use as scaling, card flow, or real fight extension once hallway frontload stops being enough.
- Upgrades matter when they change the cards the deck will cast in every hard fight. If a high-value card is already part of the deck's core line, treat an early smith as real deck development, not cosmetic polish.
- Multi-hit cards and vulnerable spread are not automatically real AoE. Before leaving Act 1, ask whether the deck can actually control multi-enemy fights or is just hoping frontload solves them.

## Failure Modes To Avoid

- taking value, then pathing like a high-roll deck anyway
- over-drafting setup without enough immediate damage
- assuming one strong pickup suddenly makes a weak deck elite-ready
- forgetting that the boss may care more about fight texture than raw card quality
- leaving Act 1 with only fair damage and no clear scaling or engine lane
- treating smiths as optional when one upgrade would materially improve the deck's most-played hard-fight card
